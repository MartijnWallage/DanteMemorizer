About

What this app does

La Selva is a tool for learning to read Dante's Divine Comedy in the original medieval Italian. The name is taken from the second line of the poem: mi ritrovai per una selva oscura — "I came to myself in a dark wood."

The first version covers Inferno, Canto I in full (136 lines). Learning is structured in three stages that unlock in sequence:

  1. Vocabulary — flashcards for 64 words drawn directly from Canto I: 30 content words (nouns, adjectives, verbs) and 34 function words (articles, prepositions, conjunctions, pronouns, adverbs). Each card shows the word's part of speech, its meaning, an example line from the text, and a note on grammar or etymology.
  2. Word order — ten individual lines, each presented as a shuffled pool of tokens. You click the words back into Dante's order. Completing an exercise shows the literal English translation and a short grammatical note.
  3. Translation — ten key passages, ranging from a single tercet to a run of nine lines. Each passage is presented as a word-for-word English prompt that follows Dante's Italian word order exactly. You write the Italian, then compare it to the original text.

Progress is saved in the browser session and resets when the session ends, or manually from the home page.

Sources

Italian text

The Italian text follows the edition of Giorgio Petrocchi:

Dante Alighieri, La Commedia secondo l'antica vulgata, ed. Giorgio Petrocchi, 4 vols. Milan: Mondadori, 1966–67.

Petrocchi's critical edition, based on the earliest manuscript tradition, remains the standard text used in scholarly work on the Commedia.

English translations

The translations used for the word-order hints, and for constructing the word-for-word English prompts in the translation exercises, are drawn from two scholarly, highly literal translations:

Robert M. Durling and Ronald L. Martinez, The Divine Comedy of Dante Alighieri, Vol. 1: Inferno. New York and Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1996.
Robert Hollander and Jean Hollander, Inferno. New York: Doubleday, 2000.

Both translations prioritise fidelity to Dante's syntax and word choice over English readability, making them well suited to the close-reading approach of this app. Where the two translations differ, the version used here is the one judged most transparent to the Italian.

Grammar and etymology notes

The notes on grammatical forms (tense, mood, agreement, apocopation, elision) and etymology (Latin sources, modern Italian cognates, borrowed forms) draw on the standard reference literature for historical Italian linguistics:

Gerhard Rohlfs, Grammatica storica della lingua italiana e dei suoi dialetti, 3 vols. Turin: Einaudi, 1966–69. (Translation of the 1949–54 German original.)
Manlio Cortelazzo and Paolo Zolli, Dizionario etimologico della lingua italiana (DELI), 5 vols. Bologna: Zanichelli, 1979–88. Second edition by Cortelazzo and Michele A. Cortelazzo, 1999.

For medieval Italian grammar specifically, and for notes on forms peculiar to the Commedia's Florentine dialect of the early fourteenth century:

Giampaolo Salvi and Lorenzo Renzi (eds.), Grammatica dell'italiano antico, 2 vols. Bologna: il Mulino, 2010.
